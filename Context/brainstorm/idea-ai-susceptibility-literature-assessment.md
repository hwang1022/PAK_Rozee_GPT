# Project Note: AI Susceptibility of Jobs and What the Existing Literature Already Covers

## The Idea As Framed So Far

One possible project is to extend preliminary work already done with the `Rozee.pk` data on classifying how susceptible different jobs are to AI. The concern is that this has become a crowded space, with many papers already building occupation-level or task-level measures of AI exposure. The obvious niche is that the `Rozee.pk` data come from a developing-country labor market, but the question is whether that alone is enough to make the project distinctive.

My assessment is that the basic version of the project, where the main contribution is a new ranking of occupations or jobs by AI susceptibility, is indeed crowded. There is now a substantial literature producing occupational exposure measures, extending them to generative AI, and applying them across countries, including low- and middle-income countries. A paper that simply says "which jobs in Pakistan are most exposed to AI?" would likely not be enough on its own.

That said, the data may still support a stronger contribution if the project moves beyond exposure ranking and studies how AI susceptibility relates to actual labor-market behavior on a two-sided platform. The strongest niches likely involve one or more of the following:

- within-occupation heterogeneity in AI exposure using full vacancy text rather than occupation codes
- changes in applicant composition or matching for more AI-exposed postings
- differences between exposure and realized labor-demand adjustment
- the interaction between AI exposure and labor-market frictions in a developing-country setting

## Overall Assessment of the Literature

The literature I found falls into four broad groups.

### 1. Foundational occupational exposure measures

These papers build exposure indices that map AI capabilities to occupational abilities or tasks. This is now a fairly established literature.

### 2. Global and cross-country exposure papers

These papers ask which countries, occupations, or demographic groups are more exposed to AI or generative AI, often emphasizing that exposure does not automatically imply displacement.

### 3. Developing-country and LMIC extensions

These papers explicitly ask how much of the AI exposure story generalizes outside high-income countries. This means the "developing-country angle" is real, but it is no longer empty terrain.

### 4. Early demand-side evidence using job postings

These papers move beyond exposure scores and try to measure actual changes in labor demand, skills demand, or vacancy composition after generative AI becomes available.

The implication is that the literature is crowded at the level of `exposure measurement`, but still thinner on `two-sided platform behavior`, `within-occupation heterogeneity`, and `developing-country microdata with actual applicants and firms`.

## Paper-by-Paper Assessment

## 1. Felten, Raj, and Seamans (2018)

### Citation

Felten, Edward W., Manav Raj, and Robert Seamans. 2018. "A Method to Link Advances in Artificial Intelligence to Occupational Abilities." *AEA Papers and Proceedings* 108: 54-57.

### What it does

This is an early methodological paper that proposes a way to map advances in AI to occupational abilities. It is one of the foundational papers behind later occupation-level exposure indices.

### Why it matters for your project

This paper is part of the reason a simple "AI exposure by occupation" paper now feels crowded. It helped create the template that many later papers follow.

### Takeaway

If your project mainly replicates this style of exercise in a new country, that is probably too narrow as a contribution.

## 2. Felten, Raj, and Seamans (2021)

### Citation

Felten, Edward W., Manav Raj, and Robert Seamans. 2021. "Occupational, Industry, and Geographic Exposure to Artificial Intelligence: A Novel Dataset and Its Potential Uses." *Strategic Management Journal* 42(12): 2195-2217.

### What it does

This paper creates and validates the AI Occupational Exposure measure and shows how it can be aggregated to industries and geographies.

### Why it matters for your project

It is probably the canonical reference for the broader occupational-exposure literature. Any paper that classifies `Rozee.pk` jobs by AI exposure would be entering a literature with a well-known benchmark already in place.

### Takeaway

Your project should not stop at constructing an exposure measure. It would need to say something about labor-market behavior that exposure datasets alone cannot show.

## 3. Felten, Raj, and Seamans (2023)

### Citation

Felten, Edward W., Manav Raj, and Robert Seamans. 2023. "Occupational Heterogeneity in Exposure to Generative AI." Working paper.

### What it does

This paper extends the exposure framework specifically to generative AI, including language modeling and image generation, and argues that highly educated, highly paid, white-collar occupations are especially exposed.

### Why it matters for your project

This means even the narrower "generative AI exposure" version of the idea is now well populated. It is not just older AI automation work that exists; there are already papers tailored to LLM-style systems.

### Takeaway

A useful response would be to shift from `which occupations are exposed?` to `how does exposure vary within occupation, across firms, or across vacancies?`

## 4. Pizzinelli, Panton, Tavares, Cazzaniga, and Li (2023)

### Citation

Pizzinelli, Carlo, Augustus J. Panton, Marina Mendes Tavares, Mauro Cazzaniga, and Longji Li. 2023. "Labor Market Exposure to AI: Cross-country Differences and Distributional Implications." IMF Working Paper 2023/216.

### What it does

This paper studies labor-market exposure to AI across advanced economies and emerging markets using worker-level microdata from the United States, United Kingdom, Brazil, Colombia, India, and South Africa. It also distinguishes between exposure that may substitute for labor and exposure that may complement labor.

### Why it matters for your project

This is important because it shows that cross-country and emerging-market variation is already in the literature. The mere fact that your data come from a developing-country setting is valuable, but not by itself enough to make the project novel.

### Takeaway

The more promising angle is not simply `Pakistan instead of rich countries`, but `Pakistan plus platform microdata, applicants, firms, and vacancy text`.

## 5. Gmyrek, Berg, and Bescond (2023)

### Citation

Gmyrek, Pawel, Janine Berg, and David Bescond. 2023. "Generative AI and Jobs: A Global Analysis of Potential Effects on Job Quantity and Quality." ILO Working Paper 96.

### What it does

This paper provides a global analysis of occupational exposure to generative AI and concludes that augmentation is likely to dominate automation in many contexts. It also highlights the strong role of clerical work and gender composition in exposure patterns.

### Why it matters for your project

This is another reason the broad "which jobs are exposed to generative AI?" question is crowded. The ILO paper already provides a global occupational mapping that many later empirical papers use.

### Takeaway

If your project stays at the occupation level, it risks looking like a local application of an already mature global framework.

## 6. Gmyrek, Berg, Kamiński, Konopczyński, Ładna, Nafradi, Rosłaniec, and Troszyński (2025)

### Citation

Gmyrek, Pawel, Janine Berg, Karol Kamiński, Filip Konopczyński, Agnieszka Ładna, Balint Nafradi, Konrad Rosłaniec, and Marek Troszyński. 2025. "Generative AI and Jobs: A Refined Global Index of Occupational Exposure." ILO Working Paper 140.

### What it does

This paper updates and refines the ILO global index using task-level data, expert input, and AI predictions. It reports that one in four workers globally are in occupations with some generative AI exposure, while exposure is much higher in high-income countries than in low-income countries.

### Why it matters for your project

This paper tightens the measurement side of the exposure literature even further. It also directly addresses one of the obvious developing-country questions by documenting cross-country differences in exposure levels.

### Takeaway

A Pakistan paper needs to go beyond refining exposure scores. The stronger question is whether AI-exposed jobs on the platform actually behave differently in terms of hiring, applicant mix, wages, or skill requirements.

## 7. Langbein, Demombynes, and Weber (2025)

### Citation

Langbein, Jörg, Gabriel Demombynes, and Michael Weber. 2025. "The Exposure of Workers to Artificial Intelligence in Low- and Middle-Income Countries." World Bank Policy Research Working Paper.

### What it does

This paper applies AI exposure measures to harmonized labor force survey data from 25 low- and middle-income countries covering 3.5 billion people. It finds that exposure is lower in poorer countries and that effective exposure is further limited by infrastructure constraints such as electricity access.

### Why it matters for your project

This paper is especially relevant because it directly occupies the "developing-country AI exposure" space. That means the developing-country setting is already a live literature, not an untouched niche.

### Takeaway

The `Rozee.pk` advantage is not just that it is in a developing country. The advantage is that it observes a platform-level labor market with actual vacancies, applicants, and employer behavior.

## 8. Green (2024)

### Citation

Green, Andrew. 2024. "Artificial Intelligence and the Changing Demand for Skills in the Labour Market." OECD Artificial Intelligence Papers, No. 14.

### What it does

This paper studies how AI exposure relates to changing skill demand in vacancies. It reports that jobs in highly AI-exposed occupations saw rising demand for emotional, cognitive, and digital skills, though the establishment-panel evidence suggests some of that demand may later begin to fall.

### Why it matters for your project

This paper matters because it moves beyond pure exposure ranking and into job-posting content. That is closer to what your data could do well.

### Takeaway

If you pursue this idea, one useful niche would be to study within-country and within-occupation variation in vacancy text, rather than assigning one score to the whole occupation.

## 9. Broecke (2023)

### Citation

Broecke, Stijn. 2023. "Artificial Intelligence and Labour Market Matching." OECD Social, Employment and Migration Working Papers, No. 284.

### What it does

This paper reviews the use of AI in recruitment and matching, including job-description writing, applicant sourcing, CV analysis, chatbots, shortlisting tools, and interview technologies.

### Why it matters for your project

It is not an exposure paper in the narrow sense, but it is highly relevant because it shows how much of the matching process itself is already being shaped by AI-related tools.

### Takeaway

This points toward a stronger `Rozee.pk` contribution: not simply classifying exposed jobs, but studying how AI susceptibility interacts with matching and screening on a labor platform.

## 10. Liu, Yu, and Wang (2025)

### Citation

Liu, Yan, Shu Yu, and He Wang. 2025. "Labor Demand in the Age of Generative AI: Early Evidence from the U.S. Job Posting Data." World Bank Policy Research Working Paper 11263.

### What it does

This paper uses 285 million U.S. job postings from 2018 to 2025 and studies the causal effect of ChatGPT's release on labor demand. It finds that postings in occupations with above-median AI substitution scores fell relative to less substitution-vulnerable occupations, with especially large declines in entry-level roles and in administrative support and professional services.

### Why it matters for your project

This is probably the closest paper to the most obvious upgraded version of your idea. It shows that the frontier has moved from exposure classification to labor-demand adjustment using job-posting data.

### Takeaway

A strong `Rozee.pk` paper would need to exploit something this U.S. job-posting paper cannot observe, such as applicant responses, realized application pools, employer screening, or developing-country institutional differences.

## 11. Klein Teeselink (2025)

### Citation

Klein Teeselink, Bouke. 2025. "Generative AI and Labor Market Outcomes: Evidence from the United Kingdom." Working paper.

### What it does

This paper studies firms and occupations in the United Kingdom from 2021 to 2025 and reports declines in employment and hiring among highly exposed firms and occupations, concentrated in junior roles.

### Why it matters for your project

This is another sign that the literature is moving quickly from exposure measures to realized labor-market outcomes.

### Takeaway

If you work on this area, the paper needs to be sharply positioned and probably should not be sold as just another exposure ranking.

## Bottom Line

The literature suggests that a simple AI-susceptibility paper would be difficult to place unless it offers something beyond a new country-level exposure index. The strongest existing contributions already cover:

- foundational exposure measurement
- generative AI exposure by occupation
- global and cross-country comparisons
- low- and middle-income-country exposure
- early labor-demand evidence from job postings

The most promising contribution of the `Rozee.pk` data would therefore be one of the following:

1. `Within-occupation heterogeneity`
   Use full job text to show that AI susceptibility varies a lot within the same occupation across firms, seniority levels, and job designs.
2. `Exposure versus matching`
   Study whether more AI-exposed jobs attract different applicant pools, different skill mixes, or different employer screening behavior.
3. `Exposure versus realized adjustment`
   Distinguish between jobs that look exposed in theory and jobs where firms actually change posting behavior, requirements, or hiring intensity.
4. `Developing-country platform dynamics`
   Use the two-sided platform data to show how AI exposure interacts with search frictions, skill constraints, and employer behavior in a developing-country labor market.

## Current Recommendation

I would not prioritize a project whose main output is a new descriptive ranking of jobs by AI susceptibility. I would keep the topic alive only if it is reframed around a question that the existing literature does not answer well with current data.

The best candidates are:

- how AI-exposed jobs change in posted requirements over time
- how applicant pools respond to more AI-exposed vacancies
- whether exposure predicts lower demand for junior workers
- whether occupation-level exposure scores miss important within-occupation variation in a developing-country labor market
